The learning proccess

Choosing what topic to learn

  • Every weekend, make a list of tools and techniques you used
  • Mark out the tools and techniques that you have historically used the most
  • Dig into that particular thing and learn how and why it works
    • If in that particular thing, you find something new, note it down.
    • When you find something new that is common within many different things in your list, learn that smaller thing.
    • Don't learn the smaller thing if it's not present withing multiple larger things that you care about

What to learn within that topic

  • Learn the algoriths, not the specific implementation
    • Example: Learn how the stack is used in bfs.
    • You can always implement the specific stack the use of that stack in whatever language you decide to use.
    • Language is exactly that: a language. Focus on the thought that goes on in your head.
    • Languages and trends will come and go.
    • Always go for the understanding, everything else is short lived and constantly changing.

How to decide if you really understand something

  • Pick a topic and make a mind map of everything you know about it
  • See if you can connect the various sub topics to each other
  • Everything is alwaus connected. If you find something not connected to anything, it's either of 3 things:
    1. Your understanding is wrong
    2. You don't understand it enough
    3. You don't know enough about other deeper things that connect to this orphan thing

Note: The things that have very few connections will get naturally forgotten. You cannot do much about them other than revising periodically without fail. It's usually okay to let these things go.

Algorithms and data structures:

  • Algorithms and data structures go hand in hand. The better way to leatn them both is to start studying an algo, and then study whatever data structure it needs.
  • Going the other way is stupid. We don't pick out all utencils and then put the we you need on the stove. We decide what we are cooking and take out the one utencil we'll need.

Personalization

The algorithms I will need will be vastly different from the algorithms you will need. Make your own list and your own arsenal.

Conventional Algorithms

  • The 3 approaches to an algorithm: (1)Divide & conquer (2)greedy (3)dynamic Questions you should be able to answer:
    1. What are they?
    2. What styles of problem solving do they represent?
    3. What are their differences?
    4. Are they mutually exclusive? Why yes or why no?
    5. Which paradigm is better for which class of problems?
